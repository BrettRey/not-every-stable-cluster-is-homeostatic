\begin{abstract}
Homeostatic property cluster theory gave natural-kinds realism a
powerful anti-essentialist form: a kind can be real because its
properties cluster through mechanisms rather than because it has an
essence \citep{boyd1991,boyd1999}. But the word \term{homeostatic} has
often carried more weight than the theory has earned. It can name
reliable co-occurrence, internal causal order, or feedback-sensitive
control. These are different explanatory achievements.

This paper treats projectibility as the target and offers a diagnostic
for how it is secured. Stable property clusters require reliable
co-occurrence with a projectible payoff. Networked kinds require internal
structure among central, derivative, and evidentially useful properties.
Control-secured kinds require a stronger pattern:
perturbation-sensitive correction that preserves a higher-scale relation
through lower-scale variation. Weinberger's account of homeostasis as
causal control supplies the guardrail
\citep{weinberger2026HomeostasisCausalControl}.

The Watt governor is the calibration case, glycemic regulation is a
harder biological extension, and English interjections are a stable pragmatic
contrast case. The result is a disciplined way to use Boyd's insight
without turning
\term{homeostatic} into a loose synonym for stable, mechanism-maintained,
or projectible.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The target is a quiet ambiguity in recent anti-essentialist kind theory.
Boyd's homeostatic property cluster framework, Slater's stable property
cluster proposal, and Khalidi's causal-network account all reject
essences. They also all aim to explain why some categories support
projection. The ambiguity concerns what secures that projection:
clustering, observed stability, internal order, or homeostatic
maintenance
\citep{boyd1991,boyd1999,slater2015,khalidi2013,khalidi2018nodes}.

The working claim is simple: not every stable cluster is homeostatic.
Treating it as such makes the framework too permissive. The correction
keeps Boyd's projectibility frame while making \term{homeostasis} more
literal and more demanding. Weinberger's causal-control account supplies
the guardrail \citep{weinberger2026HomeostasisCausalControl}.

The contribution is diagnostic. The paper does not reject HPC theory; it
separates three explanatory roles that HPC discussions often compress.
First, some profiles support projection because their properties recur
reliably. Second, some profiles support projection because their
properties occupy ordered positions in a causal or mixed network. Third,
some profiles support projection because feedback-sensitive organization
corrects perturbation and preserves a higher-scale relation. Only the
third role earns the word \term{homeostatic}.

\section{Projectibility first}

Boyd's framework works because projectibility does not require necessary
and sufficient conditions. A category can support induction when its
associated properties are not accidental, even if no property is shared
by every member. Boyd's accommodation thesis ties successful
classification to the way our inferential practices fit causal
structure, not to definitions fixed in advance \citep{boyd1991,boyd1999}.

This point is easy to weaken. If the only question is whether a cluster
recurs, then many categories will qualify too cheaply. Stable
co-occurrence may support some inference, but the paper still has to say
what can be projected from what. A stable category becomes theoretically
interesting when its profile licenses prediction, explanation, or
controlled intervention.

\section{Observed stability and invariance}

Slater's stable property cluster account usefully presses the point that
homeostatic mechanisms are not always needed for natural kindness
\citep{slater2015}. Some categories support induction because their
properties form a robust cluster, even when no corrective mechanism
returns the system to a set point. That is the first distinction this
paper needs: a stable projectible cluster may be real without being
homeostatic.

Stability is also too thin if it is left unexplained. Onishi and
Serpico argue that stable-cluster views risk losing the processes that
make some inductions rational, while strict mechanism views inherit
boundary, abstraction, and one-to-many problems \citep{onishi2022}.
Woodward's account of invariance helps sharpen the issue: explanatory
stability holds under relevant changes and interventions, not just
across space and time \citep{woodward2001}.

The first diagnostic is therefore modest. A stable property cluster should
support some projection under a specified range of contexts. The evidence
cannot be mere recurrence. It has to show that observing part of the
profile licenses a useful expectation about another part, or about the
behaviour of the system in a relevant setting.

\section{Network order without control}

Khalidi's network account addresses a different weakness. A cluster is
more than a list of associated properties. Its properties can be
ordered: some generate, sustain, constrain, or explain others. That is
an advance over a bare cluster picture because it explains why some
properties are diagnostic and others derivative
\citep{khalidi2013,khalidi2018nodes}.

Still, ordered dependence falls short of corrective control. A node in a
causal network may support projection because nearby properties stand in
systematic causal relations. That does not mean the system tracks
departures, allows lower-scale variables to adjust, and preserves a
higher-scale relation through that adjustment. Network order can explain
projectibility without being homeostasis.

The second diagnostic asks whether the profile has internal structure.
Relevant evidence includes asymmetries among properties: upstream
features, derivative features, central features, and peripheral cues. The
failure mode is causal vocabulary used for an unordered list. A paper
does not establish network order merely by redescribing co-occurrence as
causal.

I use \term{network order} broadly in the cross-domain diagnostic. In
strictly biological or mechanical cases, the ordering may be causal. In
linguistic and social cases, the relevant dependencies may also be
functional, conventional, sequential, or evidential. What matters is that
the profile has structure that explains projection. A merely associated
bundle of features is not enough.

\section{Corrective control}

Homeostatic-control-secured kinds are stricter. Kinds do not themselves
sense, compare, or correct. A kind earns the label only when its
projectible profile is maintained by a system or practice with
feedback-sensitive corrective organization. Weinberger's central lesson
is that homeostasis requires more than constancy. A controlled system may
preserve a higher-scale relation through lower-scale variation
\citep{weinberger2026HomeostasisCausalControl}.

For this paper, corrective control has four evidential marks. First, a
perturbation threatens the projectible profile. Second, a tracked
variable or relation matters to the profile. Third, a response pathway
changes lower-scale conditions. Fourth, that response preserves a
higher-scale relation that supports projection. Feedback alone is not
enough; the feedback has
to explain why the profile remains projectible under perturbation.

The biological control literature makes the same warning concrete.
Control mechanisms sense conditions, transmit signals, and alter
constraints in controlled mechanisms \citep{bichBechtel2022control}.
In organisms, such controls are integrated rather than centrally
commanded \citep{bichMossioEtAl2016,bichBechtel2022organization}.
That is much more specific than saying that a property cluster is
stable, or that its properties are causally ordered.

\section{Three diagnostics}

The proposal is best understood as a diagnostic grid, not as a simple
ladder. Homeostatic-control-secured cases will usually display stability
and network order, but the reverse inference fails. A stable cluster need
not have an internally ordered causal profile, and a network-ordered kind
need not be maintained by corrective control.

The grid turns the question around. For any proposed HPC kind, the
diagnostic asks three questions before using \term{homeostatic}. First, what
does the category let us project? Second, what structure secures that
projection: stable co-occurrence, network order, or corrective control?
Third, what evidence would move the case from one diagnostic to another?

\section{How to read the cases}

The cases below do not all have the same status. The Watt governor is
not offered as a kind. It is a calibration case for the control structure
that a homeostatic-control-secured kind would have to involve. Glycemic
regulation is a candidate biological domain where that calibration can be
applied. Interjections are a linguistic category whose stable
projectibility is clear enough to test whether stability and interactional
order license the stronger homeostatic label.

\section{Calibration case: Watt governor}

Weinberger's Watt governor is the calibration case because the control
structure is explicit. Changes in engine speed change the governor's
flyball position; that adjustment changes valve aperture; valve aperture
changes steam flow. The point is not that shaft speed is clamped. A
clamp intervention on shaft speed would sever the
incoming causal relations and destroy the control relation
\citep{weinberger2026HomeostasisCausalControl}.

The governor illustrates why \term{homeostatic} should not mean
constant. Its lower-scale variables are allowed to vary so that workload
and steam flow remain coupled in a higher-scale relation. This is the
strict pattern the diagnostic is looking for: perturbation, responsive
lower-scale adjustment, and preservation of a relation that supports
projection.

\section{Biological extension: glycemic regulation}

Glycemic regulation is useful because it is harder than the governor.
The relevant candidate is not glucose as a substance, nor a single blood
value, but the normoglycemic regulatory profile of an organism.
Blood-glucose regulation involves sensors, signals, effectors, and
organized adjustment across feeding, fasting, activity, and other
perturbations \citep{bichMossioSoto2020}. It therefore looks like a
natural biological domain in which a projectible profile might be
control-secured.

The case also keeps the diagnostic away from ordinary \term{set point}
talk. Population correlations for glucose and calcium may fit an
equilibrium model better than a defended set point
\citep{fitzgeraldBean2018}. That challenge does not make glycemic regulation
irrelevant. It makes the evidential question more precise: does
regulation merely settle into a stable range, or does it preserve a
higher-scale relation through organized lower-scale variation?

\textcite{bichMossioSoto2020} answer by moving beyond a simple
feedback-loop diagram. Glycemia regulation sits inside wider
organizational closure, where multiple constraints and processes make
glucose available to the organism while keeping concentrations within a
viable range. The lesson for kind theory is direct. A biological example
counts as homeostatic-control-secured only when the evidence shows this
organized correction, not when the variable is merely stable.

\section{Contrast case: interjections}

English interjections are the negative linguistic case. They appear
stable and projectible in use: prosodic isolation, low syntactic
integration, non-inflection, non-referentiality, and interactional force
often travel together \citep{ameka1992,norrick2009}. Observing some of
these properties licenses expectations about others. That is a
projectible profile, not a mere list.

The projectible payoff is pragmatic. Forms such as \mention{huh?},
\mention{mmhm}, and \mention{oh} support expectations about sequential
position and interactional uptake: repair initiation, continuation, and
change of epistemic state \citep{dingemanse2024}. Forms such as
\mention{wow}, \mention{oops}, \mention{hey}, \mention{well}, and
\mention{uh} invite expectations about stance, event management,
attention, transition, or delay. \textcite{norrick2009} is useful here
because he treats interjections as an open-ended class with recurrent
pragmatic-marker functions.

The list is deliberately heterogeneous. Some of these forms are analysed
as discourse markers, fillers, summonses, or response tokens rather than
as central interjections. That is part of the test. The contrast case is
not a classical kind with settled boundaries; it is a projectible
interactional profile whose kindhood is itself partly at stake.

The likely stabilizers are conventionalization, turn-taking routines,
prosodic recognizability, acquisition, register, uptake, and
metapragmatic learning. These make the category stable and useful. They
may also give parts of the profile network order: sequential position,
prosodic packaging, and syntactic non-integration are not independent
features.

The homeostasis question is stricter. For interjections, the
higher-scale relation would be an interactional profile linking turn
position, prosodic packaging, low integration, and uptake. Lower-scale
variables would include token form, intonation, sequential placement,
phonological reduction, and local conventional meaning. Perturbations
would include syntactic integration, inflection, shifted prosody,
misplaced sequential use, or uptake failure.

A homeostatic-control claim would require evidence that such
perturbations trigger coordinated correction that preserves the
higher-scale interactional relation. Repair, uptake failure, teaching,
and metalinguistic policing may sometimes provide control-like evidence.
But that evidence has to be shown. Without it, interjections belong in
the stable or network-ordered diagnostics. They are a useful case because
they make the temptation visible and then block the overclaim.

\section{Discussion}

The distinction matters for the broader HPC programme in linguistics and
social ontology. Linguistic categories may be stable, projectible, and
mechanism-maintained without all being homeostatic in the strict
control-theoretic sense. Social practices may be stabilized by records,
offices, sanctions, training, and uptake without becoming single
homeostatic control systems.

A natural objection is that social correction and metalinguistic policing
are feedback. Sometimes they are. That concession strengthens the
diagnostic rather than weakening it. It means some linguistic or social
categories may be homeostatic-control-secured, but only where correction,
repair, acquisition, transmission, and uptake form a coordinated control
architecture. Grammaticality or a contested social category may be a
better positive linguistic case than interjections. The present point is
that the evidence has to earn that upgrade.

Grammaticality shows what a positive linguistic case would require. The
higher-scale relation would be a community-sensitive pattern linking
forms, judgments, parsing expectations, correction, and transmission.
Lower-scale variables would include individual productions, acquisition
episodes, classroom correction, repair sequences, editorial practices,
and analogical levelling. A homeostatic-control claim would need evidence
that departures from the profile reliably elicit coordinated responses
that preserve the projectible relation across speakers and contexts. The
diagnostic leaves that possibility open, but it does not grant it by
default.

\section{Conclusion}

Projectibility remains the point. Mechanisms and network order matter
because they explain why projection is reliable. Homeostasis is one
important way projection can be secured, but the term should be reserved
for corrective control. The diagnostic protects Boyd's anti-essentialist
insight while preventing \term{homeostatic} from becoming a loose synonym
for stable, useful, or causally organized.
