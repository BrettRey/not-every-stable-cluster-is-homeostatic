# JGPS Audience and Tone Notes

## Reader Profile

The expected JGPS reader is a philosopher of science with broad competence
rather than a specialist in every domain the paper touches. The paper can assume
comfort with natural kinds, induction, explanation, classification, mechanisms,
and interventionist vocabulary. It should not assume detailed knowledge of
Boyd's full corpus, Khalidi's natural-kind account, Weinberger's control theory,
biological regulation literature, or the linguistics of interjections.

The reader is likely to be sympathetic to cross-domain philosophy of science,
but will want a clear reason for moving from natural kinds to biology to
language. The paper should therefore make the methodological point explicit:
the cases are not offered as a survey of domains; they test whether the same
diagnostic distinguishes different ways projectibility can be secured.

## What to Scaffold

Scaffold these points explicitly:

- Projectibility as the target of the HPC discussion.
- Why observed stability is not enough for natural-kind work.
- Why network order is stronger than co-occurrence but weaker than control.
- What Weinberger's control account adds beyond the claim that homeostasis
  involves feedback.
- Why the Watt governor calibrates control without itself being a kind.
- What the glycemia case is a candidate case of: a normoglycemic regulatory
  profile, not glucose as a substance or a single variable.
- Why interjections are a legitimate negative test: stable pragmatic
  projectibility without demonstrated coordinated corrective control.
- Why grammaticality is mentioned: it shows what a positive linguistic case
  might require.

Do not over-scaffold these points:

- The general history of natural kinds.
- A full survey of HPC objections.
- Technical details of control theory beyond what the diagnostic needs.
- Conversation analysis or interjection taxonomy beyond the features that make
  the contrast case work.
- The broader labels-to-stabilizers project.

## Tone

The target voice is controlled, diagnostic, and cross-domain. The paper should
sound like it is giving philosophers of science a usable evidential test, not
like it is policing terminology.

Preferred moves:

- State the diagnostic before the cases.
- Use short topic sentences that say what role each section plays.
- Define each specialist term at first use, then use it without apology.
- Keep the prose argumentative rather than encyclopedic.
- Use the table as the hinge and return to it in each case.

Avoid:

- Treating JGPS readers like beginners in philosophy of science.
- Assuming they know the biology or linguistics literature.
- Letting the Weinberger section become a technical excursus.
- Letting the interjection section become a linguistics paper.
- Claiming the diagnostic has classified all linguistic or social kinds.

## Section-Level Guidance

Introduction: move quickly from HPC ambiguity to the diagnostic contribution.

Boyd/Slater/Khalidi sections: give enough literature grounding to show the
paper knows the debate, but keep each source tied to projectibility.

Weinberger section: explain the control-theoretic lesson in ordinary language.
The key point is not merely feedback; it is preservation of a higher-scale
relation through lower-scale variation.

Cases section: make each case's status explicit. Governor calibrates the
control structure. Glycemic regulation is a hard biological application.
Interjections are a negative linguistic test.

Discussion: keep the door open for linguistic/social control-secured kinds
without granting the label by default. Grammaticality should appear as a
candidate test case, not as an already established positive result.
