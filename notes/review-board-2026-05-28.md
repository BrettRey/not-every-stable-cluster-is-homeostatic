## GPT-5.5 Pro Comments

The core argument is not fallacious, and it does not obviously rely on a crude strawman. The main thesis is careful: it distinguishes stable clustering, network order, and corrective control; it explicitly says the proposal is diagnostic rather than revisionary; and it repeatedly grants that stable and network-ordered kinds may be perfectly real and projectible without being homeostatic. That charitable framing protects the paper from the worst version of the strawman worry.

The places that still look vulnerable are more local. I would fix them before submission.

## 1. Possible strawman: "treating every stable cluster as homeostatic"

The sentence "treating every stable cluster as homeostatic makes the framework more permissive than it should be" risks making the opponent too blunt. The draft does not name anyone who literally holds that every stable cluster is homeostatic. Slater is presented as deliberately separating stability from the mechanisms that maintain it, and Khalidi is presented as appealing to network order rather than feedback control, so neither is an obvious target for the claim that stable clustering is simply equated with homeostasis.

The safer version is not to accuse the literature of the universal claim. Make the target a tendency in the vocabulary:

> The problem is not that anyone explicitly identifies every stable cluster with a homeostatic one. It is that the shared HPC vocabulary makes it too easy to move from stability, maintenance, or network order to the stronger homeostatic label without specifying the securing structure.

That preserves the objection while avoiding a strawman.

## 2. Possible strawman of Slater: "underlying processes are dropped altogether"

The line in section 3 is the most likely strawman: "If underlying processes are dropped altogether, as on Slater's account, the analysis loses the very thing that makes some inductions rational rather than lucky." The earlier description of Slater is more charitable: Slater separates the stability of a cluster from the various ways stability might be maintained. That is not the same as denying or dropping underlying processes altogether.

I would revise to:

> If the explanatory role of underlying processes is made optional or postponed, as stable-cluster views invite us to do, the account risks leaving underexplained why some inductions are rational rather than lucky.

That changes "Slater drops processes" into "Slater's view creates a pressure." Much fairer.

## 3. Possible strawman of Khalidi: "causal structure is retained but loosened"

The Khalidi discussion is mostly fair, but "loosened" could sound like a downgrade rather than a different explanatory strategy. The paper says Khalidi's account improves on a bare cluster picture by ordering properties into generating, sustaining, constraining, and explaining relations. It then says network order is compatible with no error signal, no tracking, and no preserved higher-scale relation. That distinction is good.

The possible weakness is that the reader may hear: "Khalidi gives only an unordered or insufficiently disciplined causal story." You do not need that. You only need: "Khalidi gives order, but order is not yet control."

Suggested adjustment:

> Khalidi's account is not too weak because it lacks order; its strength is precisely that it supplies order. The present point is narrower: ordered dependence, even when objective and explanatory, does not by itself amount to feedback-sensitive correction.

That removes any hint of a strawman.

## 4. Equivocation / false analogy: *controller* in Corbett vs controller in control theory

This is the biggest vulnerability in the complete draft. The gender-concord section says that Corbett's agreement controller and a feedback-loop controller "pick out the same structural role," and that the positive linguistic case and the governor case "share their structure."

That is too strong. In agreement theory, a controller is the element whose features determine agreement on targets. In control theory, a controller is part of a feedback architecture that compares, responds, and adjusts. Those are not obviously the same structural role. The noun in *la casa blanca* does not sense a mismatch, compare a target form with a standard, and issue a corrective signal. Speakers, parsing systems, acquisition routines, editors, teachers, or community norms may do some of that work, but the noun-controller itself does not.

So the current wording risks an equivocation:

> Corbett calls the noun a controller.
> Feedback theory calls a correcting element a controller.
> Therefore grammatical concord has the same structure as feedback control.

That inference will draw reviewer fire.

I would replace the strong claim with a weaker and more defensible one:

> The terminology is suggestive but not decisive. Corbett's controller-target relation identifies a dependency structure in agreement; by itself, it is not a feedback-control architecture. The control-theoretic question arises only when that dependency is embedded in production monitoring, repair, acquisition, or normative correction.

Then the gender case becomes plausible again.

## 5. Fallacy of composition: combining several loops into one "system"

The gender-concord section admits that online repair, acquisition, and normative correction operate at different scales. That is a good qualification. But it still says "the four marks are met" and that "the system registers departures and returns to the relation."

This risks a fallacy of composition. Because several processes each contribute to the stability of concord, the paper may appear to infer that there is a single coordinated control system securing concord. But online self-monitoring, child language development, classroom correction, copy-editing, and community norm enforcement are not automatically parts of one feedback architecture.

Suggested fix:

> The evidence does not yet show one control loop. It shows several candidate loops at different scales. A stronger case would have to specify which loop is doing the controlling, what it tracks, what counts as an error signal, and what response pathway returns the system to concord.

This would make the positive case more modest but stronger.

## 6. Hasty generalization / overstatement: "the system does not tolerate" agreement mismatch

The sentence "A mismatch is a perturbation, and the system does not tolerate it" is too absolute. Agreement systems tolerate, mask, or complicate mismatch in many ways: syncretism, semantic agreement, default agreement, hybrid nouns, agreement attraction, performance errors, dialectal variation, and repairs that fail or are ignored. The paper does later qualify the case, but the initial phrasing is rhetorically overcommitted.

A safer version:

> In canonical concord domains, mismatch is treated as a perturbation: it disrupts the projectible relation among controller and targets and is often subject to repair, rejection, or recasting.

That keeps the point without pretending agreement systems are exceptionless.

## 7. Category mistake / hypostatization: "the system registers departures"

The paper wisely says earlier that kinds do not themselves sense, compare, or correct; the bearer of control is the system or practice, not the category named. But the gender-concord section occasionally slides back into singular system-talk: "the system registers departures and returns to the relation."

That is close to the category mistake the paper wants to avoid. Which system? The speaker's production monitor? The child's learning system? The grammar? The speech community? The schoolroom? The copy-editorial regime?

A repair would be:

> In online production, speakers may register and repair departures from concord; in acquisition, learners converge on the pattern over developmental time; in normative contexts, others may reject or correct mismatches. These are different candidate control processes, not automatically one system.

This is also where the paper can distinguish *grammar as constraint* from *control as correction*.

## 8. Begging-the-question risk: defining *homeostatic* as corrective control

The paper argues that only corrective control earns *homeostatic* in the strict sense. That is plausible, especially given the appeal to Weinberger. But a reviewer could say the paper gets the conclusion by definition: it first defines homeostasis as corrective control, then concludes that only corrective-control cases are homeostatic.

The best way to block that objection is to make the dialectical status explicit. The paper is not discovering the one true meaning of *homeostatic*; it is proposing a disciplined use of the term for HPC theory. The draft already points in that direction by saying the aim is not to police a word but to make the evidential demand explicit. I would strengthen that.

Possible insertion:

> Nothing in the argument requires that every historical use of *homeostatic* in HPC theory already meant feedback control. The claim is normative and diagnostic: if the term is to add anything beyond stability, maintenance, or network order, then corrective control is the structure that gives it distinctive content.

That avoids circularity.

## 9. False trichotomy risk: stability, network order, or control

The diagnostic grid is useful, but it can read as though there are exactly three ways projectibility is secured. The abstract says the term has been used for "at least three things," which is safer, but later phrasing sometimes makes the three structures sound exhaustive.

This is easily fixed:

> The grid is not an exhaustive taxonomy of all possible securing structures. It distinguishes three recurrently conflated roles within HPC discussions: reliable co-occurrence, ordered dependence, and corrective control.

That prevents objections involving selection, drift, niche construction, institutional scaffolding, exemplar effects, path dependence, or other stabilizing processes that may not fit neatly into the triad.

## 10. Weak analogy: Watt governor to gender concord

The Watt governor is explicitly marked as a calibration case rather than a kind, which is good. The danger appears when gender concord is said to transpose the governor's structure into grammar.

The analogy works only if the comparison is not:

> governor : engine speed :: noun : adjective form

but rather:

> feedback-control architecture : shaft-speed relation :: monitoring/repair/acquisition/normative architecture : concord relation

That is, the analogue of the governor is not the noun. It is the whole corrective architecture that detects and repairs mismatches. The noun is, at most, part of the dependency relation being preserved.

I would recast the gender-concord section around this distinction.

## 11. Possible conflation: constraint vs correction

Gender concord is clearly constrained. It is less clearly controlled. The section sometimes treats the fact that a grammar requires matching forms as evidence that the system returns to concord. But a constraint is not yet a corrective pathway. A well-formedness condition can explain why mismatches are excluded from the grammar without showing that mismatches are sensed and repaired in a feedback-control process.

The draft anticipates this by discussing production repair, acquisition, and normative correction. That is the right direction. But the conclusion should rest on those processes, not on agreement morphology alone.

Suggested revision:

> Agreement morphology establishes the dependency relation. It does not by itself establish corrective control. The control claim depends on evidence from repair, monitoring, acquisition, and norm enforcement.

## 12. Overclaim about Spanish vs English pronoun gender

The contrast between Spanish concord and English pronoun gender is useful, but "where Spanish would force a recast" is probably too strong as written. Spanish has canonical concord, but actual gender use is complicated by epicene nouns, common-gender nouns, semantic agreement, pronoun choice, repair, recategorisation, and social change. The claim can stand if narrowed.

Better:

> In canonical Spanish concord domains, a shift in construal that conflicts with the grammatical gender of the controller typically requires recasting the expression rather than simply switching agreement values within the same concord relation.

That protects the contrast without making Spanish look mechanically uniform.

## 13. The interjection case is mostly safe

The interjection section is actually the least fallacy-prone. It says interjections are stable and projectible, identifies likely stabilizers, grants possible network order, and then withholds the homeostatic-control label until evidence of coordinated correction is produced.

That is a model for the gender section. The paper should make gender concord just as cautious: not "the stronger label can be earned," but "this is where the stronger label comes closest to being earned, provided the relevant control loop is specified."

## Bottom line

The main paper does not rest on a strawman. Its central distinction is strong and well protected. The fallacy risks are concentrated in two places: the characterization of Slater as dropping underlying processes, and the gender-concord argument's use of *controller*. The latter is the more serious problem. If left as is, a reviewer may say the paper equivocates between grammatical control and feedback control. If revised so that Corbettian control supplies only the dependency relation, while production monitoring, acquisition, repair, and normative correction supply the candidate feedback loops, the positive linguistic case becomes much more defensible.

## Simulated Reviewer: Muhammad Ali Khalidi

1. **One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that HPC theory should distinguish stable projectible clusters, network-ordered kinds, and genuinely homeostatic control-secured kinds, reserving "homeostatic" for cases where perturbation-sensitive correction preserves a projectible higher-scale relation.

2. **Strengths**

- The "network order without control" distinction is real and philosophically useful. The paper correctly sees that causal-network order can explain projectibility without involving error detection, feedback, or correction. The discussion around lines 177-191 is especially charitable to Khalidi: it treats causal-network order as an advance over bare clustering, not as a failed version of homeostasis.

- The paper gets the HPC projectibility priority mostly right. It explicitly says mechanisms matter because they explain why projection is reliable, not because mechanism-hunting is the point; see lines 121-134 and the diagnostic grid at lines 314-319. This avoids the common failure of treating HPC as merely "cluster plus mechanism."

- The stabilizer/controller distinction is strong. Lines 218-234 make a clean counterfactual separation between what keeps a cluster together and what detects and corrects departures from a projectible relation. That is the paper's best conceptual contribution.

3. **Weaknesses**

- The broadened use of "network order" risks becoming too permissive. Lines 200-204 expand network order beyond causal dependencies to "functional, conventional, sequential, or evidential" relations. That may be right for linguistic and social kinds, but it needs sharper constraints. Otherwise, "network order" starts to mean any structured description that helps projection. From Khalidi's perspective, the causal-network account's force comes from asymmetric dependence relations among properties; evidential usefulness alone is not enough. The paper should say which non-causal relations are genuinely ordering relations and which are merely diagnostic correlations.

- The gender concord case overclaims control. The paper's strongest vulnerable sentence is lines 467-471: Corbett's grammatical "controller" and a feedback-loop controller are said to pick out the same structural role. That is too quick. In agreement theory, the controller determines feature values; in control theory, the controller detects deviation and initiates correction. Those are not automatically the same role. The later qualification at lines 499-506 helps, but it comes after the paper has already treated concord as structurally analogous to the governor. The case would be stronger if it separated constraint, feature valuation, online repair, acquisition, and norm enforcement, then argued which one actually supplies feedback-sensitive correction.

- Projectibility is not decorative, but the case studies sometimes undercash it. The introduction and conclusion frame the paper correctly: "a profile, stabilised by mechanisms, projectible for a purpose." But in the positive cases, especially glycemic regulation and gender concord, the paper should make the projectible payoff more explicit. What exactly does the category let us predict, explain, or generalize that a weaker stable-cluster or network-order description would not? The interjection case does this better, especially lines 415-420. The concord case gives one answer at lines 490-492, but it needs to be more central: the reader should come away knowing not just what preserves concord, but what concord lets us infer.

4. **Key Question for Q&A**

If network order can be causal, functional, conventional, sequential, or evidential, what prevents the middle category from absorbing almost every useful non-random profile, and thereby weakening the contrast with both mere stable clustering and control-secured homeostasis?

5. **Verdict: Revise & Resubmit**

The core distinction is promising and the treatment of Khalidi is largely fair, but the paper needs sharper criteria for non-causal network order and a more disciplined argument that grammatical concord involves corrective control rather than structured dependence plus norm-governed repair.

## Simulated Reviewer: Yukinori Onishi

1. **One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that HPC theory should treat projectibility as the target and distinguish three ways it can be secured: stable co-occurrence, network order, and the narrower case of homeostatic corrective control.

2. **Strengths**

- The paper gets the projectibility framing basically right: mechanisms are not treated as the prize; they explain why a category supports projection. This is clearest in [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/Not_Every_Stable_Cluster_Is_Homeostatic/main.tex:121), where the paper says mechanisms matter because they explain reliable projection.

- The stabilizer/controller distinction is useful and philosophically productive. It gives the paper a real diagnostic target: not "is there maintenance?" but "is there perturbation-sensitive correction that preserves a higher-scale projectible relation?"

- The interjection case works well as a negative case. It shows charity toward stable, useful, mechanism-maintained linguistic categories while refusing to inflate them into homeostatic kinds without evidence of coordinated correction.

3. **Weaknesses**

- The diagnostic risks becoming stipulative at the exact point where it needs to be non-stipulative. "Network order" is defined broadly enough to include causal, functional, conventional, sequential, and evidential dependence, which may reintroduce the looseness the paper criticizes. The paper should say what observable contrast separates mere stable co-occurrence from network order in linguistic/social cases.

- The handling of Slater is a little too quick. The claim that Slater drops underlying processes and thereby loses what makes induction rational rather than lucky may be fair, but as written it risks begging the question against mechanism-sceptical HPC. The paper should more explicitly say what Slater can still explain, what he cannot explain, and why "specified projection plus invariance" is an improvement rather than a return to Boyd under another name.

- The gender concord case is the pressure point. Corbett's "controller" is a grammatical source of agreement features; a feedback controller senses error and initiates correction. The paper's claim that these pick out "the same structural role" is too strong. Repair, acquisition, and normative correction operate at different scales, and the paper admits this, but it still needs to show that they form one coordinated control architecture rather than three ordinary stabilizers. Otherwise the positive case may simply restate: "agreement is enforced," not "agreement is homeostatically controlled."

4. **Key Question at Q&A**

What prediction about gender concord is licensed by treating it as control-secured that would not already be licensed by treating it as a stable, network-ordered grammatical dependency with repair and norm enforcement?

5. **Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit.** The paper has a strong projectibility-first frame and a promising diagnostic, but the positive control case and the response to mechanism-sceptical HPC need sharper, non-vocabulary-based tests before the central distinction is fully earned.

## Simulated Reviewer: Naftali Weinberger

1. **One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that HPC theory should reserve "homeostatic" for projectible kinds secured by perturbation-sensitive causal control, not for every stable or network-ordered property cluster.

2. **Strengths**

- The governor is correctly treated as a calibration case, not as a kind: the paper explicitly says it is "not put forward as a kind" but as a reference point for the control structure a homeostatic kind would need to involve.
- The control lesson is largely right: the paper understands that control is not constancy, but preservation of a higher-scale relation through lower-scale variation. This matches the checked Weinberger article's point that the governor exploits lower-scale feedback to produce a higher-scale workload/steam-flow relation. Source checked: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10539-026-10018-8
- The HPC framing passes the projectibility audit. Mechanisms are presented as explaining why projection works, not as the point in themselves. The abstract, section 2, diagnostic table, and conclusion all keep the third clause alive: profile, stabilized by mechanisms, projectible for a purpose.

3. **Weaknesses**

- The gender-concord case overreaches. The claim that "the structure here is the governor's, transposed into grammar" and that Corbett's controller has "the same structural role" as a feedback-loop controller is too strong. A grammatical controller is a dependency source; it need not sense error, compare against a target, or dynamically adjust lower-scale variables. The paper should either downgrade concord to network order plus control-like repair, or specify one actual feedback loop with timescale, tracked variable, perturbation, response pathway, and controlled relation.
- The concord section mixes control loops operating at different scales. Online self-monitoring, child acquisition, and community norm enforcement may all stabilize concord, but they are not automatically one coordinated controller. The paper notices this qualification, but still concludes that the four marks are met. For JGPS, that needs tightening: pick the strongest loop and show why it is control, not mere constraint, selection, learning, or policing.
- The intervention/clamping discussion is accurate but too compressed. The paper correctly says clamping shaft speed would sever the governor's control relation, but it should distinguish bad clamp interventions on regulated/endogenous variables from useful perturbations to workload or demand. Otherwise the paper risks sounding anti-interventionist when the better point is that control models have intervention ranges and "let-go" conditions.

4. **Key Question at Q&A**

In the gender-concord case, what exactly is the controller: the morphosyntactic dependency, the speaker's online monitoring system, the child's learning dynamics, or community-level correction, and on what timescale does it preserve the projected relation?

5. **Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit.** The central distinction is strong and the governor/control machinery is mostly handled well, but the positive linguistic case currently trades on a loose analogy between agreement "control" and causal control that the paper itself is designed to prevent.

## Simulated Reviewer: Marc Ereshefsky

1. **One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that HPC theory should treat projectibility as the target of kind claims and distinguish three ways projectibility can be secured: stable co-occurrence, network order, and the narrower case of homeostatic corrective control.

2. **Strengths**

- The paper understands Boyd's point well: mechanisms matter because they explain why projection is reliable, not because mechanism-talk by itself confers kindhood. This is explicit at lines 121-127 and in the conclusion at lines 567-572.

- The stabilizer/controller distinction is a genuine advance. Lines 209-235 cleanly separate "what keeps the cluster together" from "what corrects perturbations to preserve a projectible profile," which is exactly the distinction HPC discussions often blur.

- The interjection case works well as a negative case. It grants stability, pragmatic usefulness, and some network order, then refuses the stronger homeostasis label without evidence of coordinated correction. That is philosophically disciplined.

3. **Weaknesses**

- The paper risks over-identifying kindhood with projectibility, even though it avoids over-identifying homeostasis with projectibility. Lines 121-127 say a projectible category is already doing "much of the work a realist about kinds cares about," and the diagnostic grid asks first what the category lets us project. That is right for Boyd-style natural-kind realism, but too narrow for classification generally. The paper should say more explicitly that it is diagnosing *projectible realist kind claims*, not all legitimate scientific classifications.

- Descriptive and classificatory aims are underdeveloped. Many scientific taxonomies are valuable because they map variation, stabilize communication, support measurement, mark historical relations, or organize inquiry before they support strong projection. The paper gestures toward interest-relativity at lines 157-162, but it should give pluralism more space: some categories may be good classifications without being strong projectible kinds, and that should not look like a failure.

- The gender concord case overreaches at the point where the paper needs most caution. Lines 467-471 claim that Corbett's grammatical "controller" and a feedback-loop controller pick out the same structural role. That is too quick. Agreement control is dependency-licensing; feedback control is perturbation-sensitive correction. The later qualifications at lines 499-506 help, but the paper should weaken the identity claim and say that concord is *analogous to* or *candidate evidence for* control only if processing, acquisition, or normative repair supplies the feedback architecture.

4. **Key Question for Q&A**

Are you offering a diagnostic for *natural kindhood as such*, or only for *Boyd-style projectible kind claims*? If the latter, where do descriptive, historical, pragmatic, and classificatory kinds fit in your pluralist picture?

5. **Verdict**

**Revise & Resubmit.** The core diagnostic is promising and philosophically useful, but the paper needs a clearer pluralist framing and a less compressed argument for the cross-domain generality of the control category.
