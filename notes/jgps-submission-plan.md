# JGPS Submission Plan

## Target

Primary target: Journal for General Philosophy of Science.

Rationale: JGPS is the best fit for the paper's actual shape: general
philosophy of science, philosophy of biology, philosophy of linguistic and
social categories, and a diagnostic about projectibility. The journal's scope
explicitly includes general philosophy of science and specialized philosophies
of biology, social sciences, engineering disciplines, and the humanities.

Official pages checked 2026-05-28:

- Aims and scope: https://link.springer.com/journal/10838/aims-and-scope
- Submission guidelines: https://link.springer.com/journal/10838/submission-guidelines
- Ethics and disclosures: https://link.springer.com/journal/10838/ethics-and-disclosures
- Publishing options: https://link.springer.com/journal/10838/how-to-publish-with-us

Audience and tone notes: `notes/jgps-audience-tone.md`.

## Article Type

Submit as a compact research article, not as a discussion.

JGPS article category:

- Full-length research article.
- Peer-reviewed by at least two reviewers.
- Around 10,000 words, but no strict upper limit.

JGPS discussion category:

- Brief comment on articles published in JGPS or on recently published
  contributions in other leading journals.
- Peer-reviewed by one reviewer.
- Maximum 5,000 words.

This paper is not mainly a comment on one article. It uses Weinberger,
Onishi and Serpico, Slater, Boyd, Khalidi, and the interjection case to build
a diagnostic. That belongs in the article category even if the final article is
shorter than the modal JGPS article.

Working length target: 5,500--7,000 words. Shorter than this risks reading as
underdeveloped for a research article; longer than this risks turning the
diagnostic into a survey.

## Submission Requirements

JGPS requires or requests:

- Online submission through Springer Editorial Manager.
- Editable source files at every submission and revision; LaTeX is accepted.
- Title page with title, author name, affiliation, corresponding author email,
  and ORCID if available.
- Abstract of 150--250 words, with no undefined abbreviations or unspecified
  references.
- 4--6 keywords.
- Statements and Declarations section, including competing interests.
- Acknowledgements on the title page.
- Human accountability for the final manuscript.
- LLM use documented in the Methods section or another suitable place if it
  involved more than AI-assisted copy editing.
- References should include full DOI links where available.
- Research content is double-anonymous peer reviewed.

Current manuscript implications:

- Create an anonymized submission manuscript without author name, ORCID,
  contact footnote, acknowledgements, or LLM declaration in the main file.
- Create a separate title page containing identifying information,
  acknowledgements, LLM declaration, competing interests, and any funding
  statement.
- Keywords added to the manuscript: natural kinds; homeostatic property
  clusters; projectibility; homeostasis; causal control; linguistic categories.
- Convert DOI fields or final reference rendering to full DOI links if needed
  for the production version.

## Expected Reader

JGPS readers can be expected to know:

- The broad stakes of natural kinds, scientific classification, induction, and
  explanation.
- The idea that Boydian HPC theory is anti-essentialist and tied to
  projectibility, even if not every reader knows the details.
- General philosophy-of-science idioms such as invariance, mechanism,
  explanation, and intervention.
- At least a passing sense of debates over special-science kinds and
  cross-domain classification.

JGPS readers should not be expected to know:

- Weinberger's 2026 control-theoretic account in detail.
- Iwasaki-Simon causal ordering or the technical control-theory apparatus.
- Biological regulation literature on glycemia, set points, organizational
  closure, or internal control.
- The linguistics of interjections, conversation analysis, repair initiators,
  continuers, or pragmatic markers.
- Brett's broader labels-to-stabilizers project.

## Tone and Voice

Use the voice of a short, diagnostic philosophy-of-science article:

- Direct, argumentative, and conceptually careful.
- Enough signposting to orient cross-domain readers.
- No textbook survey of natural kinds, homeostasis, or interjections.
- No disciplinary gatekeeping; explain specialist tools as they enter.
- Avoid sounding like the paper is correcting terminological etiquette only.
  The claim is evidential and methodological: what must be shown to earn a
  homeostatic-control interpretation of projectibility.

The paper should feel like it gives readers a usable test. The diagnostic table
is the hinge, not a summary accessory.

## Scaffolding Strategy

The introduction should do three things quickly:

1. Define the problem: HPC talk compresses stability, network order, and
   corrective control.
2. Define the target: projectibility is what kind claims are trying to secure.
3. State the contribution: the paper offers diagnostics for how projectibility
   is secured.

The body should scaffold in this order:

1. Boyd/HPC: enough to make projectibility central.
2. Slater/Onishi-Serpico: why stability alone is too thin.
3. Khalidi: why network order is stronger than clustering but weaker than
   control.
4. Weinberger: why homeostasis is not constancy and why direct clamping can
   destroy control.
5. Diagnostic grid: make the paper's test explicit before the cases.
6. Cases: governor as calibration, glycemic regulation as hard biological
   extension, interjections as negative linguistic test.
7. Grammaticality: brief positive linguistic possibility, enough to show the
   diagnostic is not biased against language.

Pedagogy level: moderate. Explain the governor and interjection examples in
plain terms, but do not over-teach Boyd or natural kinds. JGPS readers need the
paper to connect literatures; they do not need a primer.

## Revision Priorities for JGPS

1. Expand the Boyd/Khalidi/Onishi-Serpico setup without making it a survey.
2. Clarify exactly what counts as the projectible profile in each case.
3. Add a short methodological paragraph explaining why cross-domain cases are
   legitimate for a general philosophy-of-science venue.
4. Give Weinberger enough detail to show that the paper is not mining one
   slogan from the article.
5. Add a slightly fuller grammaticality sketch as a possible positive
   linguistic case.
6. Keep the conclusion focused on what the diagnostic lets philosophers of
   science do: distinguish projectible stability, ordered dependence, and
   corrective control.
